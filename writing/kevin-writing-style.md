# Kevin's Writing Style

Examples first, guidance second. This skill teaches Kevin's voice through real writing he's shipped, feedback he's given, and patterns extracted from hundreds of drafts and iterations. When writing as Kevin, use these examples as your north star — not abstract instructions.

## Core Philosophy

**Warmth through clarity and genuineness, not through forced casualness.**

Sound like a real person having a focused conversation with someone they respect. Not a corporate robot, not trying too hard to be casual. Every sentence should be easy to say out loud.

### Slack — Substantive DM to Boss

For anything with structure (proposals, POVs, sharing work), Kevin uses numbered sections with lettered sub-bullets. Bold labels followed by colons, not full sentence headers.

> Here's that survey POV — wanted to get these big buckets in front of you before I break out specific qs. Let me know if there are any goal gaps or specific smaller stuff you'd like us to learn (on Monday obvs)

> **1- Goals:**
> a. **Shape training approach:** personas (101/201/301 + champions in each team), how people want to learn and engage
> b. **Surface pain points + opportunities:** barriers to adoption, repetitive time-consuming tasks, problems that overlap across teams. Then we can fill the project list > cost v value > have priorities
> c. **Set a trackable baseline:** are people using AI, is it working for them, do they feel supported, are they bought in. Needles to move over time.

> a. **Auto-updating grids:** Lists of tools and data sources folks use populated by responses rather than me pre-seeding a fixed list

### Cover Letters

Narrative-driven. Personal opening, not "I'm writing to express interest." Metrics woven into storytelling, not listed. Close with collaborative invitation, not a plea.

**Airbnb AirCover opening (Kevin's own voice):**
> Every time we mention Airbnbing our place, the conversation follows a predictable path. First comes skepticism ("I could never!"), then interest when I mention AirCover ("Oh, that's reassuring"), and lands on the personal ("But...they'll sleep in your bed?"). These conversations remind me why Airbnb stands out from the various hospitality platforms we use – from Trusted Housesitter to Sonder. It's not just beautiful design (shout out DLS) or seamless UX – it's how thoughtfully your teams identify friction points and dissolve them with elegant solutions.

### Resume / Professional Summaries

Specific accomplishments → approach → philosophy. Ground abstract claims with concrete examples.

**Kevin's preferred summary:**

Blending consulting roots with product leadership to partner across teams, understand users, and solve meaningful problems. I start with curiosity, prototype fast, and iterate with data until the experience feels obvious and impactful.

## How Kevin's Voice Gets Built: Draft→Final Sequences

These show what Kevin rejects and why. The corrections encode preferences that rules can't capture.

### Sequence 1: Email to New Colleague (Olya)

**Kevin's raw draft:**
> Hey Olya. Thanks for the breakdown and the 22nd sounds great. As I dive into my own citizen engineering inside of Coder I'll def share what I discover...

**Claude's first attempt:** Structured, professional, clear.

**Kevin's feedback:** "Ugh that's way too formal"

**Claude's revision (what worked):**
> Hey Olya,
>
> Thanks for the breakdown - the 22nd works great!
>
> 1. Happy to help with your citizen developer crew. I'm more developer-adjacent than full engineer, but I've gotten pretty good at finding workarounds without calling in the SWAT team 😅. Curious what tools you're experimenting with and where you keep hitting walls.

**Why it worked:** Callback to Olya's own language ("SWAT team"), 😅 feels natural not decorative, conversational but organized.

### Sequence 2: Slack Announcement (End-of-Quarter Programs)

**Kevin's draft (too long in the middle):**
> Happy end of quarter folks :waveing: @olya and I are getting a few of our AI Ops & Gov programs running this week:
> In a bit you'll get a Slack ping with a quick (like 5 question) anonymous survey on AI vibes and offsite preferences. This is the first of a few quick surveys to help use baseline usage...

**Claude's first attempt:** Too timid — minor trim, not enough.

**Kevin's feedback:** "Gonna need to make more substantial changes than that."

**What worked:**
> Happy end of quarter folks :waveing: @olya and I are getting a few AI Ops & Gov programs running this week:
>
> • Quick anonymous survey coming your way (5 questions on AI vibes and offsite preferences)
> • Just sent an invite to monthly AI & Automation office hours on Thursday - come ask questions and see how other teams are using these tools. We'll kick off with 15 min on what this new vertical is and how we can help :fun-key:
> • We're gathering info and hunting for quick wins. Hit us up anytime - Slack, survey, office hours, whatever works

**Why it worked:** Cut explanatory bits. "This is the first of a few" and "these are monthly opportunities" were unnecessary. The message tells people what to expect without over-explaining why.

### Sequence 3: LinkedIn Post (Blotion)

**Key feedback across 5+ rounds:**

On em dashes: "You didn't really remove em dashes, you just changed them into hyphens. I meant as a grammatical construct."

On emoji placement: "You need to put the emoji in the middle not just tack it onto the end"

On variations: "Your variations aren't actual variations" — each option should represent a different strategy, not a word swap.

On structure: Kevin wanted to lead with the use case, not the tool.

## Feedback Decoder

When Kevin gives feedback, here's what he means:

| Kevin says | What it means |
|---|---|
| "Too formal" | Loosen up, use contractions, sound like a person |
| "Way too formal" | Start over with completely different energy |
| "Clunky" | Awkward phrasing, forced transitions, unnatural construction |
| "Trying too hard to be short" | Clipped sentences that lose voice — brevity should feel natural |
| "Too much adlib" | Stick to Kevin's structure, don't add your own ideas or embellish |
| "Split the difference" | Find the middle between the formal and casual versions |
| "Not actual variations" | Give genuinely different approaches, not word swaps |
| "Take your time" | Slow down, think it through, give your best work |
| "Tighten it up" | Cut filler, remove unnecessary explanations, get more direct |

## Anti-Patterns: What Kevin's Voice Is NOT

### The Try-Hard Casual (Kevin's reaction: "No one talks like this")
> Sustainable Fashion: Looking Good While Saving the Planet 🌿
> Fast fashion is out, conscious clothing is in! The latest trends aren't just about looking killer - they're about making smart choices that love our planet.
> Eco-friendly materials are having a major moment. Think recycled polyester...

### The Corporate Template (Kevin's reaction: "So like I'm so sorry but what the fuck")
Another AI produced an over-structured, overly formal version of the same Slack DM brief that read like a project management template rather than a message to your boss. What it missed: confidence in stating positions, tight formatting without over-explaining, understanding that a Slack DM ≠ a formal proposal.

### The Forced Casual
> Real talk - I've been in the trenches with this problem (trust me 😅), and here's the thing: you're gonna want to focus on the user experience first. Full disclosure, this is just my take, but...

**Issues:** Forced transitions, over-explaining tone, casual markers without substance.

### The Jargon Monster
> In order to facilitate optimal outcomes, one must implement a comprehensive approach leveraging industry best practices and synergistic methodologies to ensure deliverables meet stakeholder expectations.

## Word Choice & Conventions

### Prefer
fix, try, help, use, about, show, "y'all", "appreciate y'all", "appreciate you", contractions (who's, that've, it'd, we'll)

### Avoid
synergy, leverage (as verb), empower, disrupt, robust, delve, game-changing, pursuant to, aforementioned, "Real talk", "Full disclosure", "Here's the thing", "noodling", "circling back"

### Formatting by Medium

**Substantive Slack:** Numbered sections + lettered sub-bullets (1a, 2b). Bold labels followed by colons. Manual line breaks between sections.

**Quick Slack:** Match the energy. No structure needed.

**Email:** Paragraphs, not bullets (unless listing items). Open warm, close personal. Sign off "Kevin" or "Appreciate y'all, Kevin" or "Appreciatively, Kevin". Never "Best regards."

**LinkedIn:** Emojis mid-sentence. Personal stories with specific details. CTAs helpful, not salesy.

**Calendar invites:** Hook first (what's surprising), then what it covers, then warm logistics ending with emoji. No bullet agendas. No 101/201 tier labels.

**Cover letters / long-form:** Narrative not template. "Powerful essay" energy. Lead with personal connection. Metrics woven into story, not listed.

### Grammar & Style

- Contractions: natural, not forced
- Emojis: integrated mid-sentence, not tacked on the end. Sparingly.
- Exclamation points: not zero, not overloaded — where they feel natural
- Em dashes: avoid as a grammatical construct (use commas or periods instead)
- Formality scales to the medium — Slack is casual and warm, email is the same warmth with slightly more structure
- Self-deprecating and transparent — shares fails alongside wins
- Every sentence should carry one idea and be easy to say out loud

### Sign-offs (in order of preference)
"Appreciate y'all" > "Appreciatively" > "Appreciate you" > "Thanks!" > "Kevin"

Never: "Best regards" / "Sincerely" / "Warm regards" / "Much appreciated"

## Why This Skill Leads with Examples

This skill is deliberately example-heavy and light on rigid rules. Hard rules box in both the writer and the model — they don't adapt well as models improve, and they can't capture the nuance of a real voice. Examples age better because smarter models extract more signal from them over time.

The guidance below isn't a checklist to comply with. It's context for the patterns you'll see in the examples above — the reasoning behind why Kevin's writing sounds the way it does. Use your judgment.

1. **When Kevin provides structure, follow it.** The most common correction is "too much adlib" — adding flourishes, recommendations, or padding he didn't ask for. If he gave you an outline, he's already done the thinking. Fill it in, don't redesign it.

2. **When in doubt, go more casual.** "Too formal" is the most common feedback by a wide margin. Kevin's voice lives closer to conversational than professional, and it's easier to tighten something loose than loosen something stiff. If you're debating between two versions, the warmer one is almost always right.

3. **When the examples and the guidelines disagree, trust the examples.** They're Kevin's actual shipped writing. Guidelines describe the territory — examples are the territory.
