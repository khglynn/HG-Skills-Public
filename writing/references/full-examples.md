### Email to Former Boss (Roos) — Catch-Up + Stats Request

**Context:** Light catch-up email to former boss. Hit key points: hope SHRM went well, want a catch-up call, any RS success stats for job hunting, open to contract work.

**Kevin's raw draft:**
> Roos! Hope you had a good SHRM and that CEO/RS life is solid. Couple months on I'd love to have a catchup call if you're game. Or sale numbers over time? Hope those metrics look good! I'm open to contract work if there's anything y'all need. Insight mining, validation setup, project outlining, whatever. **Claude's first draft:** Too buttoned up. Every sentence polished, lost the casual energy.

**Lesson:** Kevin's voice lives between formal and overly casual. "Split the difference" is a recurring direction.

### Tecovas Offer Counter — High-Stakes Negotiation

**Context:** Counter offer. $175k → asking for $192k + later start date.

**Key feedback moments across many rounds:**

- "Phrases like these are still kind of clunky" (on "I'd love to lock that in and sign within the next couple days if possible")
- "the great great is meh. Can we be more specific?"
- "And really no !s? Ha" (some exclamation points are fine)
- "I feel like the excited to get started is in contradiction with my 1 month ask"
- "Some of these just feel like they're trying too hard to be short. They don't sound like me"

**What Kevin actually sent:**
> Morning y'all,
>
> Thank you for the opportunity to join the Tecovas team. If the convos I've had are any indication, it'll be a great place to do some great work.
>
> I've got a few questions, but none will materially impact my ask. I'd like to go 10% higher on the salary ($192k base) and to start Thursday, January 22nd. That brings me closer to my target range and gives me time to wrap up some projects and travel over MLK weekend.
>
> [continued with questions about role level, hardware, strike price, PTO, discount]

**Lessons:**
- Kevin wants specificity over platitudes ("If the convos I've had are any indication" > generic praise)
- "Trying too hard to be short" is as bad as being too long
- Questions should sound natural: "What do hardware options look like for the tech team?" not just "Hardware?"

## WORD CHOICE & GRAMMAR CONFIRMED PREFERENCES

- "low-key" (hyphenated)
- "rollout" (one word as noun), "roll out" (two words as verb)
- Spell out "JavaScript" on resumes, "JS" okay in conversation
- Uses contractions naturally (who's, that've, it'd)
- "y'all" — yes, always
- Uses :) and :p in casual emails to friends
- "Appreciate you" and "Appreciatively" as sign-offs
- "Fingers crossed" as a closer
- Avoids "workflow" when possible — prefers more specific language
- No em dashes as grammatical construct
- Emojis mid-sentence, not end-of-sentence

## TONE DESCRIPTIONS (From Early Analysis)

### Casual Kevin
Warmly professional but deliberately casual. Informal elements like "hey," "y'all's," and emojis to soften messages. Self-aware, gently self-deprecating. Professional enough to be taken seriously, personal enough to feel human.

### Fun Kevin
Personally confident and genuine — leading with character traits rather than qualifications. Conversational but professional. Using concrete examples but keeping them brief and punchy. Comfortable in expertise but doesn't take himself too seriously.

### Business Kevin
Clear and concrete. Active verbs (built, rebuilt, created, shipped) rather than jargon. Specific numbers and outcomes. Technical details when relevant without overdoing terminology. Direct statements of impact without excessive modifiers.
